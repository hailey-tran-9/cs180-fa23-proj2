Project 2 | Fun with Filters and Frequencies

Overview

The goal of Project 2 was to explore different image filtering methods, frequencies, and blending techniques. This included derivative and Gaussian filtering, image sharpening, and Gaussian/Laplacian stacking.

Approach

Filters

Finite Difference Operator

I took the difference operators (Figure 1) and convolved them over the cameraman image to get its partial derivatives. To get the gradient magnitude image, I calculated the square root of the squared sum of the x and y partial derivatives (Figure 2). Then I binarized the gradient magnitude image using np.where to filter out the edges.

Derivative of Gaussian (DoG)

First, I created a Gaussian kernel using cv2.getGaussianKernel with a specified kernel size and sigma value. I then took this kernel and outer multiplied it by its transpose to create a 2D Gaussian kernel. To reproduce the Gaussian filtered version of the partial derivatives, I convoluted the 2D Gaussian kernel over the cameraman image. Then I convoluted the product over the difference operators. For the following Gaussian gradient magnitude and edge filtered image, I repeated the same steps I took in the Finite Difference Operator approach but replaced the values with their Gaussian filtered versions.

Differences

The regularly convoluted images are brighter and sharper, compared to the gaussian convoluted images which are dark and smooth. Also, the lines of the regular edge filter is much thinner than the gaussian edge filter.

Frequencies

Image “Sharpening”

Note: Something I got stuck on for a long time— throughout the whole project— was figuring out how to produce colored images. To do this, I needed to get the 3 separate color channels of the image. A very useful function is cv2.split (it returns the channels in the order: b, g, r)!

I then convoluted the Gaussian filter over each channel to get the low frequencies/low-pass filtered image. Using np.dstack, I recombined the color channels to produce the colored, low frequencies. To get the high frequencies, I subtracted the original image by the low frequencies.

I sharpened the image by scaling the high frequencies by a scalar (alpha) and adding it to the original image.

Hybrids

For the hybrid images, I extracted the low and high frequencies from the image and added them together. The alignment and rotation was done with the provided starter code. Each set of hybrid images had attuned cutoff-frequencies.

Blending

In order to blend images, I made a Gaussian and Laplacian stack. The Gaussian stack followed the same process as image sharpening. First, I got the 3 separate color channels from the original image, convoluted each one, and stacked the products to extract the low frequencies. This process was repeated multiple times with different kernel sizes and sigma values. To get the Laplacian stack, I iterated through the Gaussian stack and subtracted the current layer from the previous layer until I reached the last layer (which is kept the same).

I created 1 mask image that had a white shape in the spot that I wanted an image overlaid upon another. Then I got a second mask image by subtracting 1 by the mask image.

Then I generated the Gaussian and Laplacian stacks of the two subject images, and the Gaussian stack of the mask images. Using the respective pairs (image #1 with mask #1, image #2 with mask #2), I multiplied each layer of the images’ Laplacian stacks with each layer of the masks’ Gaussian stacks. This created a stack for both images progressively being blended. For each layer, I added the two products together to create a stack of the progression of the final image. Finally, I added all of the layers I created together to complete each image (the final result, blend of image #1 and #2, blend of image #1, and blend of image #2).

Both of these images were taken on campus! They capture recent discoveries I’ve made.

The coolest thing I learned from this assignment was that blending requires a mask and that the images are merged in layers!

Conclusion

The most important thing I learned from this project is that convolution is a powerful operation and that the Gaussian filter is very versatile. But in order to produce colored images, you have to split the color channels and convolve each one!
